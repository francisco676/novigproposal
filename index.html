GA
Gal Aga Co-Founder & CEO at Aligned · 92,000 followers 3w

I have to come clean. LinkedIn is not for all CEOs. It drives ±60% of our ARR. We get 50M+ views/year across internal + Partner/UGC posts. But in Q1, I stopped posting regularly, and leads from LinkedIn dropped 50%.

We're still growing fast, but navigating this was painful.

I've talked a lot about the upside of founder-led growth.

But this is the part we don't talk about enough.

Founder-led growth has a cost.

And most founders underestimate it.

DON'T invest in LinkedIn if you:

1. CAN'T COMMIT TO THE TIME TAX

Writing isn't the hard part. The context switching is. Whenever I post, it steals focus for 60-90min mid-workday. I make final edits, reply to comments, and engage in other posts. Add that to 6-9hr writing/week... Big commitment.

2. DON'T HAVE THE MENTAL BANDWIDTH

You're never "done." Every walk, dinner, or meeting becomes a potential post. The effort isn't the hard part. Staying inspired and excited to write is. And when you stop publishing for a while, restarting feels heavier than starting from zero.

3. CAN'T COMMIT TO CONSISTENCY (FIRST MONTHS, THEN YEARS)

This one hurts most. Momentum is fragile. Miss a few weeks, and the drop is steeper than you expect. In Q1, I posted 50% less. Almost 50% was old content. And I almost didn't engage. My reach crashed, and we had to compensate for it.

4. DON'T TRULY ENJOY BUILDING RELATIONSHIPS (CAN'T SKIP THIS)

Posting is the easy part. What breaks people is everything around it. The replies. The DMs. Showing up in others' feeds. Skip it and your audience feels it immediately. They stop coming back. And so do the leads.

5. NEED CLEAN ATTRIBUTION AND TIDY ROI SPREADSHEETS

It won't show up as last-touch. ~60% of our ARR self-report LinkedIn - but you can't prove the full impact in the CRM right away. Your board will ask. You won't have a clean answer. If you need the dashboard before you commit, you'll quit before it works.

This isn't ads you can treat like a transaction you just pump $$ into.

And it's not PR where you just optimize for the message.

It's trust, built in public.

That takes time and attention.

For me, that's 10+ hours/week.

And having to continuously "growth hack" when things break.

Founder-led growth works.

But it has a cost - and we're not talking about it enough.

How we work

Three pillars. None of them are AI slop.

Quality on LinkedIn comes from three things working together. Most ghostwriters get one. We use AI to maximize context and research, not to write the posts. Real humans on our team do the final writing.

01

LinkedIn craft

Hooks that land in prediction-market and sports-fintech feeds. Storylines that hold. The platform mechanics most posts get wrong. We write to this medium specifically.

02

Category depth

Day-one fluency in prediction markets, CFTC regulation, and peer-to-peer sports trading. We track what Polymarket, Kalshi, and BettorEdge post so we can take Jacob's angle on it first.

03

Jacob's voice

Every post sounds like him. Not generic CEO. Not ChatGPT. His press-release precision, his analytical edge, his partner-announcement energy. Jacob stays the editor, not the typist. He reviews drafts on his schedule, marks them up, or approves them; how much he edits is his call. What we take off his plate is everything else: the research, the drafting, the formatting, the monitoring.

The reason most CEO content fails is that the writer knows LinkedIn but not the category, or knows the category but not Jacob. Our system handles all three at once, which is how we ship three high-quality posts a week without Jacob losing an hour to it.

Questions buyers actually ask

What you're probably wondering. Plain answers.

I get the timing matters. But why not wait until we have the CFTC designation in hand, or until the national launch?+
When the designation lands, reporters covering it will call Tarek Mansour at Kalshi by default; he's the CFTC-regulated name already in their Rolodex, and he's been posting consistently for years. Jacob needs to be the name they reach for instead. That doesn't happen the day he starts posting. It happens after months of showing up consistently. An audience takes six months to build; you can't compress that into the two weeks before football season. Start now and Jacob owns the narrative when the window opens. Start after the launch and he's catching up to a story someone else already framed. Section 03 has the math on what one quarter of silence costs in concrete terms: the regulatory narrative, the partnerships that stay outbound, the launch campaign that lands cold.
Why not just have ChatGPT or Claude do this?+
You can. Most LinkedIn content already does. Originality.ai's 2025 study of 3,368 posts from 99 influential profiles flagged 53.7% of long-form LinkedIn content as likely AI-generated. That's exactly the problem. The reason posts hitting Jacob's feed feel forgettable is they all read the same. We use AI heavily for the unsexy work — scraping competitor posts, tracking what category leaders publish, keeping the Novig context graph fresh — and real humans on our team write every post. The system gives us category fluency on day one; the humans give Jacob something the algorithm hasn't already seen 10,000 times this week.
Does LinkedIn even reach Novig's buyers? Sports bettors are on X and Reddit, not LinkedIn.+
Sports bettors are on LinkedIn too — plenty of them. But the question is which audience moves the needle for Novig right now. League executives vet potential partners on LinkedIn before they pick up the phone. VCs researching the Series C look at what Jacob is saying publicly. Policy makers writing CFTC comment letters cite founders they've been reading. Senior engineers evaluate companies by their founders' public voice. Novig's $75M round came from the right people in the right rooms knowing what Jacob was building. LinkedIn is where that circle expands, systematically, at no additional cost per relationship.
What if Jacob doesn't want to post, or hates the drafts?+
Nothing ships without Jacob's approval. Jacob stays the editor: if a draft isn't right, he marks it up and we rewrite. Week 1 is explicitly calibration; the first batch is about learning his voice, not just shipping posts. We've worked with founders who are very particular about their writing, and that's not a problem. It's exactly the signal that the content is going to matter. The 90-day window is designed so the continue-or-walk call gets made on real data: does it sound like him, does it land in the right feeds, does it produce the inbound he wanted?
Why not just run this internally with our marketing team?+
Sammy and Nikhil already do a version of this — surfacing topics, throwing Jacob ideas, putting angles in front of him. The reason it hasn't produced a consistent cadence isn't effort. Realistically, an internal marketer gives this 10–20% of their week. Founder LinkedIn done at the level Novig needs requires full focus: voice-matched drafting, hook engineering, algorithm timing, format decisions, and category monitoring. These are craft skills that only compound when someone is doing this and nothing else. That's what we do all day, every day, for a specific set of founder profiles. The national launch is about to consume every hour Sammy and Nikhil have. There's also a structural tension in internal ghostwriting: a marketing team's instinct is to promote the company, where Jacob's LinkedIn is most valuable when it builds his personal thesis on regulation and category design. Those goals overlap but they're not the same, and the tension tends to produce product-announcement cadences, which is exactly where Jacob's profile already sits.

Most agencies sell engagement metrics. We measure ICP-relevant audience growth. Most agencies write generic operator-life posts in the voice you'd expect from a LinkedIn template. We start from a Context Graph specific to Jacob's category, backstory, and the angles Novig already owns — the no-vig thesis, the CFTC regulatory arc, the founding story with Kelechi. The cadence is the product, not the volume.
